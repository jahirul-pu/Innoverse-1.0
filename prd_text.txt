1. Executive Summary 
Innoverse Technologies is a Bangladesh-based, online-only e-commerce platform selling retail 
gadgets and electronics to enthusiasts and everyday customers. Unlike traditional retailers, 
Innoverse combines safety stock for fast-moving items with on-demand sourcing for the 
rest of its catalog — an internal operational model that stays invisible to the customer, who 
simply experiences a standard, reliable stock and delivery flow. Because there is no physical 
storefront, the website itself is the product — customer experience, trust, speed, and clarity are 
the primary competitive levers. 
 
This document expands the founder's original requirements into a complete PRD: functional 
requirements for the storefront and admin panel, non-functional requirements, 
Bangladesh-specific payment and logistics considerations, an order lifecycle, success metrics, 
risks, and open questions to resolve before design/development begins. 
 
 
2. Business Overview 
2.1 Business Model 
-​
Online-only, direct-to-consumer e-commerce (no physical retail presence). 
-​
Source-on-demand + safety stock: most products are sourced based on market trend, 
demand, and supplier availability rather than bulk pre-stocked inventory, while 
fast-moving/high-demand SKUs are backed by a safety stock buffer to keep them 
reliably available (§6.3). This is an internal operational detail — the storefront always 
presents a simple, standard stock experience (In Stock / Out of Stock) to the customer, 
never a "made to order" or sourcing-based narrative (§5.5). 
-​
Revenue from product margin; potential future revenue from featured listings/brand 
placements once the platform matures. 
2.2 Objectives 
1.​ Launch a fast, trustworthy, mobile-first storefront that converts browsers into buyers. 
2.​ Present a simple, standard stock and delivery experience to the customer — In Stock / 
Out of Stock, honest delivery timelines — while sourcing and safety-stock operations 
stay entirely behind the scenes. 
3.​ Support the payment and delivery realities of Bangladesh (BanglaQR, COD, nationwide 
Pathao coverage). 
4.​ Give the admin/ops team the tools to manage a fast-moving, trend-driven catalog without 
chaos, and to keep product returns low. 
2.3 Target Audience 
-​
Tech & gadget enthusiasts: early adopters looking for unique, niche, or hard-to-find 
electronics not commonly available in local markets. 
-​
Everyday customers: buyers looking for common gadgets/accessories (chargers, 
earbuds, smart devices, home electronics) with reliable service and fair pricing. 
-​
Primarily mobile-first users — the majority of e-commerce traffic in Bangladesh comes 
from mobile devices, so mobile UX is not optional polish, it's the primary experience.

4. Assumptions & Constraints 
-​
Business will maintain safety stock for fast-moving/high-demand SKUs and source 
other items on demand behind the scenes (§6.3). The storefront always uses standard 
stock language (In Stock / Out of Stock) — sourcing is an internal operations concern, 
never a customer-facing narrative. 
-​
Primary market is Bangladesh; primary delivery split is Inside Dhaka vs. Outside 
Dhaka, each with different cost/time expectations, fulfilled entirely through Pathao. 
-​
Payment at launch is limited to Cash on Delivery and BanglaQR — no separate card 
gateway or individual MFS integrations. COD will likely remain the dominant method, so 
the platform must be designed to reduce COD risk (fake/failed orders), not just accept it. 
-​
Displayed prices are VAT-inclusive; VAT is not itemized separately anywhere in the 
customer-facing experience. 
-​
No minimum order value — checkout is available at any cart total. 
-​
Guest checkout is allowed, but the flow nudges customers to log in or create an 
account so they have a record of their order — it does not gate checkout behind login. 
-​
Team has not yet finalized tech stack, budget, or timeline — this PRD is scope-first and 
stack-agnostic, with a recommendation in §10. 
-​
Requirements will evolve iteratively during design — this document should be treated as 
a living artifact, versioned as it changes.

5.9 Payment Page 
Kept deliberately simple — two options at launch: 
 
-​
Cash on Delivery (COD). 
-​
BanglaQR: Bangladesh Bank's interoperable national QR payment standard. The 
customer scans one QR code at checkout using whichever banking or mobile financial 
services app they already have — bKash, Nagad, Rocket, or a participating bank app — 
and pays directly from their own account. Because it's interoperable by design, this 
single option covers all the major digital payment rails without the platform needing 
separate bKash/Nagad/card integrations. See §9.1 for integration notes. 
-​
Addition — COD risk mitigation: optional small non-refundable advance/deposit (via 
BanglaQR) on COD orders, and/or SMS/call verification before dispatch, since 
return-to-origin (failed COD delivery) is a known cost driver in Bangladesh e-commerce. 
-​
Clear breakdown: item total, discount, delivery charge, grand total. Prices are 
VAT-inclusive and VAT is not shown as a separate line item.

7. Order Lifecycle / Status Workflow 
Order Placed → Confirmed → Processing → Ready to Ship →  
 
Shipped (Pathao) → Out for Delivery → Delivered → Completed 
 
           ↘ Cancelled (by customer/admin, pre-shipment) 
 
           ↘ Returned → Refunded / Store Credit 
 
           ↘ Failed Delivery / RTO → Re-attempt or Refund 
 
-​
Each transition should trigger a customer notification (§5.13) and be visible on the 
tracking timeline (§5.11). 
-​
"Processing" is a single neutral status covering whatever happens internally — 
picking, sourcing, packing, QC — so operational detail never surfaces to the customer, 
while delivery estimates (§5.8) still keep them honestly informed on timing. 
 
 
8. Non-Functional Requirements 
8.1 Performance 
-​
Target page load under ~2–3 seconds on mobile 4G; optimized/compressed images 
(especially important for a gadget catalog with many product photos). 
-​
Lazy-loading for images below the fold. 
8.2 Security & Compliance 
-​
SSL/HTTPS across the entire site. 
-​
Prefer a hosted/redirect payment flow from the BanglaQR acquiring partner (customer 
redirected to a secure payment page to scan and pay) rather than handling any banking 
credentials directly on the platform. 
-​
Secure authentication (hashed passwords, optional OTP), rate-limiting on login/checkout 
to prevent abuse. 
-​
Customer data protection in line with Bangladesh's Digital Commerce Operation 
Guideline and general data-protection best practice. 
8.3 Scalability 
-​
Architecture should handle traffic spikes during promotional campaigns (flash sales, 
festival seasons like Eid) without degradation. 
8.4 SEO 
-​
Clean URLs, meta tags, structured data (schema.org Product markup) for rich search 
results, sitemap.xml. 
8.5 Accessibility 
-​
Reasonable baseline accessibility (readable contrast in both dark/light modes, alt text on 
images, keyboard-navigable menus). 
8.6 Browser/Device Compatibility 
-​
Latest versions of Chrome, Safari, Firefox, Edge; iOS and Android mobile browsers, 
given mobile-first traffic. 
8.7 Availability 
-​
Target uptime (e.g., 99.5%+), with monitoring/alerting on downtime, especially during 
active promotions.

9.2 Shipping & Logistics 
Pathao is the sole shipping/logistics partner at launch, covering both Inside Dhaka (faster, 
cheaper) and Outside Dhaka (nationwide) delivery, consistent with the checkout's delivery-zone 
requirement. 
 
Recommendation: Integrate Pathao's merchant API for automatic status sync (tracking 
numbers, delivery status flowing directly into the order timeline in §7) rather than updating 
statuses manually. Revisit adding a second courier only if Pathao's coverage or performance in 
a specific region becomes a real bottleneck — not before. 
 
Operational note: Return-to-origin (failed COD delivery) is a known cost driver in the 
Bangladeshi market regardless of courier. Consider phone/SMS order verification before 
dispatch and/or a small advance payment on COD orders above a certain value to reduce 
fake/failed orders. 
9.3 Regulatory Notes 
-​
Be aware of Bangladesh's Digital Commerce Operation Guideline (from the Ministry 
of Commerce) governing online business practices, refund timelines, and payment 
escrow-style protections — worth a legal review before launch, particularly for the Terms 
& Conditions, Return Policy, and Payment Policy pages. 
-​
Displayed prices are VAT-inclusive and VAT is not itemized separately in the 
customer-facing experience; confirm the specific treatment and rate with a local 
accountant to ensure invoices remain compliant even though the breakdown isn't shown 
to the customer.

14. Open Questions / Decisions Needed 
Decided (as of this revision): payment is COD + BanglaQR only; shipping is Pathao only; 
guest checkout is allowed with a login nudge; there's no minimum order value; prices are 
VAT-inclusive and VAT isn't shown separately; safety stock will be maintained for 
fast-moving/high-demand SKUs. 
 
Still open: 
 
1.​ Which BanglaQR acquirer/bank partner to use (e.g., SSLCommerz's Bangla QR product

15. Proposed Visual Style Direction 
Since there's no existing brand identity yet, this is a proposed starting point — grounded in the 
product itself rather than a generic e-commerce template — for your designer/developer to react 
to and refine. 
15.1 Concept: "The Spec Sheet" 
Position Innoverse visually as the technician, not the hype-seller. The direction borrows its 
visual language from the objects the audience already trusts and reads fluently: datasheets, 
multimeter readouts, PCB silkscreen, spec tables. Every color and label carries functional 
meaning (a stock status, a price, an order state) rather than existing purely for decoration — 
which also reinforces the platform's promise of clear, simple stock and delivery information, 
without ever surfacing internal supply-chain detail to the customer (§5.5). It should feel precise, 
quietly confident, and a little bit workshop — not loud, not luxury, not another blue-and-white 
marketplace template.

15.4 Layout Notes 
-​
Grid-based product listings; generous whitespace; thin 1px hairline dividers between 
sections (like a PCB trace or a spec-table row) instead of heavy drop shadows. 
-​
Minimal border-radius — sharp-ish corners on cards and buttons (4–6px), reinforcing the 
"instrument panel" feel rather than a soft, plush consumer-app look. 
-​
Every product card carries a small status dot (green = in stock, grey = out of stock) next 
to a monospace price. The same dot-plus-label pattern reappears in the order-tracking 
timeline using blue for in-progress states (Processing, Out for Delivery) — one 
consistent visual system for "where things stand," everywhere on the site.

15.6 Motion 
Restrained by default: a subtle lift + shadow on card hover, a soft pulse on the blue "Out for 
Delivery" indicator in the order-tracking timeline to imply it's actively updating, snappy (not 
animated) page transitions. Respect prefers-reduced-motion. No large gradient washes or 
scroll-triggered fireworks — the personality lives in type and the status system, not in animation. 
15.7 What This Deliberately Avoids 
To keep this from reading as a generic template, it steers away from the current default 
AI-design clichés: no warm-cream-and-serif-terracotta look, no 
near-black-background-with-a-single-acid-green-accent look, and no dense 
broadsheet/hairline-newspaper layout. It also avoids the generic "big blue trust-badge 
marketplace" look most regional e-commerce sites default to — the goal is for Innoverse to look 
like it was built by people who understand the products, not like a template with a logo swapped 
in. 
 
 
16. Steps to Reduce Product Return Rate 
Returns on electronics are typically driven by five root causes: unclear expectations, defects, 
wrong item shipped, "didn't know how to use it," and simple change-of-mind. Each has a 
concrete, low-cost fix: 
16.1 Set accurate expectations before purchase 
-​
Real product photography from multiple angles (not just manufacturer stock images) — 
customers should see the actual item, actual color, actual scale. 
-​
Complete specs table on every product page (§5.5): dimensions, compatibility, box 
contents, warranty terms — the details customers rely on when they can't physically 
inspect the item. 
-​
Short demo/unboxing video for higher-complexity gadgets, where feasible. 
-​
Genuine, unedited customer reviews and photos — curated-only reviews erode the trust 
they're meant to build. 
16.2 Catch defects before they ship 
-​
Quality-control checklist for every unit with a battery or electronics inside: power-on test, 
physical inspection, accessory count, before packing. 
-​
Photo/video proof of the item at the point of packing, attached to the order in the admin 
panel (§6.5) — this protects both the business and the customer in any dispute, and is 
especially useful for COD orders where there's no card-payment paper trail. 
-​
Protective, purpose-appropriate packaging (anti-static bags, cushioning) to prevent 
transit damage — cheap insurance against a costly return. 
16.3 Get the right item to the right person 
-​
Barcode/SKU scan verification at the packing stage to catch picking errors before 
dispatch. 
-​
Selected color/variant carried through cleanly from cart → order → packing label, so 
what's picked always matches what was ordered. 
16.4 Support customers before they decide to return 
-​
A short quick-start guide included with the product, plus a link to setup help/FAQ specific 
to that item — many "defective" returns are really "didn't know how to use it." 
-​
A responsive support channel (phone/WhatsApp) that can walk a customer through 
setup or basic troubleshooting before a return is initiated. 
-​
For genuine faults, offer a replacement or guided fix before defaulting straight to a 
refund. 
16.5 Reduce COD-related "returns" at the doorstep 
-​
Order verification call/SMS before dispatch, especially on higher-value orders — a large 
share of doorstep refusals are simply orders the customer forgot about or never fully 
committed to. 
-​
Optional small advance deposit on COD orders flagged as higher-risk (§6.6), which 
meaningfully cuts down casual/fake orders. 
16.6 Design the return policy to reduce ambiguity 
-​
A clear, simple return window (e.g., 7 days) stated in plain language, with explicit 
conditions — unopened vs. opened, defective vs. change-of-mind — since ambiguity is 
what drives both customer disputes and policy abuse. 
-​
Favor exchange or store credit over cash refund for change-of-mind returns where 
reasonable, protecting margin while still being fair to the customer. 
16.7 Close the loop with data 
-​
Log a return reason on every returned order (§6.5), not just the fact that it was returned. 
-​
Review return rate by product/category regularly (§6.12) — a SKU with a persistently 
high return rate usually points to a fixable root cause: a misleading photo, an unclear 
spec, a packaging weak point, or a genuinely faulty batch. Fix the cause, not just the 
individual return.
